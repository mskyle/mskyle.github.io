Fun With Has_many Through:

Sep 18th, 2013 | Comments

tl;dr: When you’re using has_many through: in Rails, the order of your has_many statements matters. If you’re using a join table with a through: association, declare the associations for the join table ABOVE the declarations for the child table.

Today I learned a big lesson about using has_many with join tables! I’m finally getting to actually build the rails app I’ve been planning since the beginning of Launch Academy and right now I’m doing a first draft of some of the forms.

Basically in my app people record hiking trips they’ve taken – they can indicate who they hike with and what mountains they’ve hiked. Trips* and mountains have a many-to-many relationship (one hiking trip could involve multiple mountains, and of course mountains can be hiked more than once), which I’m keeping track of with a join table (trip_mountains).

Last night, after some angst caused by the way I was validating the mountain and the trip in the trip_mountain model (hint: rather than validating trip_id and mountain_id, validate the presence of the trip and the mountain). So today, knowing what I’d done wrong yesterday, I decided to add users to the form (so that you can indicate who’s on the hike with you).

That’s when the PG null errors started. I couldn’t get any of my trips to save because Rails wasn’t generating a valid entry for the trip_mountain join table. I could force create a trip_mountain in the rails console if I supplied a trip id and mountain id, but I couldn’t create one as part of the trip creation, which was the whole point.

Anyway, something had gone wrong with my associations. But what? And how? I hadn’t messed with them at all!** Finally, in frustration, I copied over the associations from a working project of @heroiceric’s. They looked EXACTLY THE SAME as the associations I had just commented out. EXACTLY THE SAME. But, it turned out, they were in the opposite order.

Here’s how I fixed the problem. I went from:

has_many :mountains, through: :trip_mountains, inverse_of: :trips
has_many :trip_mountains, inverse_of: :trip

to:

has_many :trip_mountains, inverse_of: :trip 
has_many :mountains, through: :trip_mountains, inverse_of: :trips

And, ta-da, everything was fixed. Woo-hoo.

Earlier in the day when I added my user associations to the model, I shifted things around a little to neaten them up. Originally the join table associations were grouped together and then underneath I had the child table associations; I decided it would be neater to have the user-related associations next to each other and the mountain-related table associations together. That would have been fine, except I must have reversed the order of the declarations in the process. Which turns out to matter.

It does make sense to declare the join table associations above the child table associations – obviously, the child associations don’t work without the join table. And in all the documentation for Associations they always do the join table above the child table.

But I, with my infinite faith in Rails, just assumed Rails would be able to figure that out. It can’t! Declare your parent associations above the child associations or else Rails won’t know how to get the right ids into the right join tables and you’ll get PG null errors! Learn from my mistake!

* I have to call them “trips” instead of “hikes” because of the hike gem.

** This turned out to be not entirely true.
